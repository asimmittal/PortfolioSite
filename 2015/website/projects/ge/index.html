Problem

Radiologists spend hours on end reviewing medical images (x-rays, MRIs etc.) on their terminals. A large part of their time is spent in dealing with interruptions on the phone or in person, making them less productive due to excessive context switching. Also the tools they use to handle communications with other stakeholders are outdated and not integrated with the tools they use to get their work done.

Solution



Synaptic is a collaboration tool that integrates with the radiologists' current RIS/PACS system (used to read and review images) and provides them instant access to their colleagues. They can share case files with the colleagues and even invite them for a collaborative viewing of an image with useful features to highlight and mark images during the discussion.

Process - Overview

The final concept for Synaptic was built over four iterations of the user centered design process. With each iteration, we focused in on the final feature set. Each iteration ended with testing our prototypes with users. Finally, we delivered the research, wireframes, a consolidated design spec and an interactive prototype to GE Healthcare over a period of 8 months.

Research

We began our research by interviewing radiology residents at University of Pittsburgh to get a general idea of how radiologists typically their day. After doing our homework, we set out on a journey across four major hospital systems in the United States, shadowing and observing radiologits, imaging technicians, nurses and other stakeholders in the radiology ecosystem.


We synthesized our findings, developed workflow models and highlighted breakdowns in the radiology workflow. Next, we brainstormed ideas which might help radiologists overcome some of these breakdowns. Through a careful aggregation process, we narrowed down thirty small ideas into ten larger ideas. We rated these based on their impact/achievability ratio and pitched them to our clients at GE Healthcare to gauge their enthusiasm.

Prototyping

Following the research phase, the team began putting ideas on paper. We prototyped various features through sketches on paper and with continuous feedback from our users, refined them to cleaner and more structured wireframe. These wireframes were used to build an interactive prototype (built in WPF and C#). As we tested and iterated over our features, we combined paper mockups of new designs with the existing interactive prototype to give our users a glimpse of how the product was changing.


Finale

We delivered our research findings to GE Healthcare in the form of an exhaustive report, an interactive prototype and a design specification. You can read the entire report on my slideshare.
